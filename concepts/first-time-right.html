First Time Right

Do it properly the first time; the shortcut you skip now is the rework you pay for later — with interest.

There's a persistent illusion that speed and quality trade off — that doing it right is the slow path. Over any real horizon the opposite holds: the corner you cut doesn't disappear, it moves downstream and grows. A half-done job becomes a bug, a bug becomes an incident, an incident becomes a rebuild — and each stage costs more than doing it once, carefully, would have. First time right is the discipline of paying the full price up front, when it's smallest: think it through, build it properly, check it before you move on. It isn't perfectionism — perfectionism polishes what didn't need it; this is about not leaving known weakness behind you. The honest caveat: "right" is contextual, and there are throwaway things that genuinely don't warrant it. But for anything you'll live with, the cheapest moment to get it right is the first one.
